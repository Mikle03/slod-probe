## Major prompt 1 — literature summary (verbatim)

```text
So, since the first deliverable is a Literature Summary, which covers subtopics such as probing methodology and what probing classifiers can and cannot tell us about representations, hierarchical structure in embeddings and what is known about how abstract and concrete concepts are encoded, weak supervision approaches and how document structure can replace manual annotation, and applicability to SLoD, meaning which insights are most relevant to detecting abstraction level in scientific text, we first need to understand that the literature mentioned above gives us the introductory foundation for this whole topic. The papers on probing and representation analysis, such as Tenney, Das, and Pavlick’s “BERT Rediscovers the Classical NLP Pipeline” and Belinkov’s “Probing Classifiers: Promises, Shortcomings, and Advances,” together with the papers on hierarchical and hyperbolic representations, such as Nickel and Kiela’s “Poincaré Embeddings for Learning Hierarchical Representations” and Vilnis and McCallum’s “Word Representations via Gaussian Embedding,” and also the papers on scientific text and weak supervision, such as Lo et al.’s “S2ORC: The Semantic Scholar Open Research Corpus” and Ratner et al.’s “Snorkel: Rapid Training Data Creation with Weak Supervision,” all serve as background for understanding probing classifiers as diagnostic tools. These tools can be used to test whether a specific property can be predicted from a model’s internal representations of the input. This is a useful technique because it gives us a way to gain insights into large language models and pretrained encoders, and these papers explain how we can use this tool, how we can apply it, and how we should analyze the results. Going deeper into the first subtopic, probing methodology, it is important to explain how the whole mechanism of probing classifiers works. There is some pretrained model, and we want to understand whether it can give us a certain insight about a particular field or a particular aspect of textual information. As we can see from the literature, researchers used this kind of method, for example, to understand whether a model can distinguish different parts of speech or encode morphological, syntactic, semantic, and other linguistic properties. In our case, the task is to understand whether we can detect abstraction level in scientific text based on frozen embeddings. Continuing with the functionality, the process works in the following way. We have a certain input, and we want to classify it in order to understand whether we can decode particular information from the model’s representation. So we take the input and produce frozen embeddings through an encoder, or through a pretrained model, in order to create a stable representation for this input text. Then a separate classifier is trained on top of these representations. After we receive the internal representation of the input and train the classifier, it is important that the classifier should not be too complex. Otherwise, it may give higher accuracy, but at the same time it may simply memorize certain patterns in the text. Therefore, in order to reveal whether a particular property is decodable, we use simple classifiers, such as a linear probing classifier. So, after we get these frozen embeddings, we use them as input for a linear probing classifier, and the classifier tries to classify these embeddings into the categories connected to the property that we want to investigate. In our project, this property is the semantic level of detail. In other words, we want to know whether we can determine the abstraction level of the text by classifying a given text span into macro, meso, or micro levels of abstraction. Manually, we can often understand which type a text belongs to based on certain observations, such as what vocabulary is used, how general or detailed the text is, and whether it contains broad claims or technical details. In our case, if the probing classifier performs well, this suggests that the target property is extractable from the representation. The most careful interpretation is that the property is decodable from the embedding space, but not that the model definitely understands and uses this particular property in its output generation. This distinction is very important because probing classifiers show whether information can be recovered from representations, but they do not directly prove that the original model uses this information in its own reasoning or generation. At the same time, there are certain risks. High results may actually correlate with other properties of the text, not with semantic abstraction itself. Therefore, the literature says that it is important to conduct additional control analysis. For linear probes, as mentioned above, we usually use simple models such as logistic regression or linear SVM. Also, probing results should be compared against baselines. For example, we can use a majority-class baseline because it shows what performance can be achieved without using embeddings meaningfully. In this project, we will also use a length-control experiment and cross-domain evaluation, so that the model does not simply rely on certain words that appear more often in one context or one part of the text. Moving to the second question, hierarchical structure in embeddings, we can say that research shows representations can encode more than simple semantic similarity. They can also include hierarchy, asymmetric relations, specificity, uncertainty, and other more complex structures. This is relevant for our project because SLoD is naturally organized at different abstraction levels. It is a hierarchical way of presenting information. The more detailed the text is, the more it moves toward lower-level, specific information, while higher-level representations may be summaries, broad claims, or more general descriptions of the same content. At the macro level, text may contain broad claims, research contributions, conclusions, and general motivation. The more detailed the text becomes, the more concrete details appear, such as numbers, methodological steps, model details, experimental settings, metrics, and so on. Nickel and Kiela’s work on Poincaré embeddings explains that hyperbolic space is well suited for representing tree-like or hierarchical structures. However, we are not going to use hyperbolic embeddings in our project. In this sense, SLoD can be treated as a practical form of semantic hierarchy in scientific writing. Another paper by Vilnis and McCallum discusses Gaussian embeddings, which are another format of representation and help reveal other aspects and properties of embeddings, such as uncertainty and specificity. This is relevant because abstract and concrete concepts may differ in how broad or specific they are. Therefore, the SLoD probe tests whether this kind of abstraction-level information is already recoverable from frozen language model embeddings. Then, speaking about weak supervision, we first need to define weak supervision. It is a way to generate training labels using particular rules or signals instead of manual annotation. If we do not have large manually labeled datasets to train the model, then we need to get this data from somewhere in order to train the classifier and tell it what each example represents. Manual labeling can be very expensive because it requires human labor. Therefore, Ratner and others introduced Snorkel as a framework for creating labeled data through labeling functions. Labeling functions are programmatic rules that assign labels to data points. These rules are not perfect, but they can still create useful training data. They are not perfect because research papers do not always use the same table of contents or the same section names. Because of this, it is difficult to rely only on programmed keywords or section labels for labeling data. In our case, we are interested in different levels of text abstraction. In this weak supervision framework, we can use criteria such as the table of contents, section names, certain words that appear more often in abstract texts than in detailed texts, and also certain metric or technical parameters that are expressed through methods, experiments, and detailed results. These sections often contain operational, technical, and numerical details. For example, in our case, title, abstract, introduction, and conclusion sections can be assigned the macro label. Section lead paragraphs can be assigned the meso label because they often introduce the purpose or content of the section. Methods, experiments, and detailed results paragraphs can be assigned the micro label because they usually contain operational, technical, and numerical details. In this way, we can apply section-based rule functions in order to obtain data for training. The Semantic Scholar Open Research Corpus introduces a large corpus of scientific papers with metadata, abstracts, references, and structured full text. Therefore, it is useful for our project because it preserves scientific document structure. This helps us extract text spans and label them automatically based on their position in the paper. Finally, we come to the applicability to SLoD. We already started discussing this earlier, but here we should add the information that does not simply repeat the previous points. The SLoD task combines three research ideas: probing classifiers, hierarchical representations, and weak supervision from structured scientific text. Probing methodology provides the experimental framework, meaning it tells us how to build the experiment. Weak supervision gives us the labeling strategy, meaning that it helps us obtain training data and test data. Hierarchical representation research gives us the conceptual background for thinking about abstraction level as something that may be reflected in embeddings. In our design, we will keep the encoder frozen, meaning it is not trained on the same data for the SLoD task. The encoder only produces embeddings. The probing classifier is trained on the training embeddings and then tested on unseen test embeddings. We will also consider several main risks. The strongest result would be high performance in three conditions: in-domain, cross-domain, and length-controlled. In-domain performance shows whether the signal is recoverable or extractable within one scientific domain. Cross-domain performance tests whether the signal generalizes beyond domain-specific vocabulary. Length-controlled performance tests whether the probe is using semantic abstraction rather than simply relying on non-semantic features such as text length. After this, we need to explain the limitations. Abstracts, introductions, methods sections, and other parts of scientific papers may differ in length. If length is not controlled, the classifier may learn length-based patterns. It is also worth talking about section-specific vocabulary. Macro sections may contain phrases such as “we propose,” “we introduce,” “our contribution,” or “the purpose of this work.” Micro sections may contain words such as “table,” “accuracy,” “dropout,” “training,” “experiment,” and numerical values. Another risk is domain leakage, because if the model is trained and tested only on one domain, it may learn domain-specific writing patterns. Therefore, cross-domain evaluation helps test whether the learned signal transfers to another field. It is also important that the scientific papers and documents used for training should not leak into the test dataset. Text spans from the same paper should not appear in both training and testing. Otherwise, the classifier may simply learn the vocabulary, topic, or writing style of a particular paper rather than learning a more general SLoD signal. Therefore, the dataset should be split by paper ID, not just randomly by text span.
```

## What was accepted or modified

AI-assisted code and prose were reviewed and modified where necessary. Important project decisions included treating structural labels as weak proxies, splitting data by paper ID, using frozen SciBERT embeddings with attention-mask-aware mean pooling, using a separate fixed-length control dataset, and initially reporting cross-domain evaluation as unavailable rather than fabricating a QASPER domain. A later extension added a separately sourced PMC BioC biomedical external test set and explicitly treated the result as combined domain-and-corpus transfer.
